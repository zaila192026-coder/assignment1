Sickle Cell Disease: Understanding the Genetic Blood Disorder

Introduction

Sickle Cell Disease (SCD) is a hereditary blood disorder that affects millions of people worldwide, particularly those of African descent. It is characterized by the production of abnormal hemoglobin, the protein in red blood cells responsible for carrying oxygen throughout the body. The disease can cause severe pain, anemia, infections, and other serious health complications.

What is Sickle Cell Disease?

Sickle Cell Disease is an inherited condition caused by a mutation in the gene responsible for producing hemoglobin. Normally, red blood cells are round and flexible, allowing them to move easily through blood vessels. In individuals with SCD, the red blood cells become rigid and crescent-shaped, resembling a sickle or a crescent moon.
These abnormal cells can block blood flow in small blood vessels, reducing oxygen delivery to tissues and organs.

Causes of Sickle Cell Disease

Sickle Cell Disease is caused by a genetic mutation in the hemoglobin beta (HBB) gene. The disease is inherited in an autosomal recessive pattern, meaning a child must inherit the defective gene from both parents to develop the condition.
  • If both parents carry the sickle cell trait (AS), there is:
    • 25% chance of having a child with normal hemoglobin (AA)
    • 50% chance of having a child with sickle cell trait (AS)
    • 25% chance of having a child with sickle cell disease (SS)
